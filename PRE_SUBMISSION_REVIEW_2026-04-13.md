# Pre-Submission Referee Report

**Paper**: Metacognition biases information seeking in assessing ambiguous news
**Authors**: Valentin Guigon, Marie Claire Villeval, Jean-Claude Dreher
**Date**: 2026-04-13
**Review Standard**: Leading Field Journal

---

## Overall Assessment

This paper studies whether confidence in veracity judgments predicts information seeking when people evaluate ambiguous news headlines. Its main strength is the clear experimental focus on ambiguity, the deliberate attempt to separate confidence from accuracy, and the use of multiple convergent analyses to support that framing. The most important issue is that several central claims still read as if confidence has been shown to causally drive information seeking, when the evidence is a strong within-task association plus mediation modeling rather than an exogenous manipulation of confidence. A leading field journal would likely view the paper as promising and interesting, but still in need of tighter claim discipline and a sharper defense of the information-seeking interpretation.

**Preliminary Recommendation**: Revise before sending to referees

## 1. Contribution & Referee Assessment

## Agent 6: Contribution Evaluation

### Part 1 - Central Contribution
The paper claims that when people assess ambiguous news, their subjective confidence does not track actual accuracy, yet that same confidence strongly predicts whether they seek more information.

This is a meaningful and timely contribution because it links misinformation-relevant judgment, metacognitive calibration, and information seeking within one controlled design. The strongest novelty is not merely that ambiguous news is hard to classify, but that the paper tries to separate confidence from accuracy and then show that confidence remains behaviorally consequential. The contribution is better described as **Significant** than transformative: it opens a useful line of work and offers a credible experimental demonstration, but it does not yet definitively settle the mechanism by which confidence alters information seeking.

### Part 2 - Design and Credibility
The design is an incentivized within-subject online experiment with ambiguous headlines, confidence reports, and willingness-to-pay for more or less follow-up information. That is appropriate for studying associations among judgment, confidence, and information seeking, but it is not by itself a clean causal design for claims that confidence drives later choices.

[CRITICAL] Design-to-claim gap | The paper repeatedly frames confidence as a driver of information seeking, but confidence is measured rather than randomized or otherwise exogenously shifted. A skeptical reviewer will ask whether both confidence and information demand are jointly determined by a third factor such as perceived ambiguity, topic-specific priors, or task engagement.

[CRITICAL] Information-seeking outcome validity | The "avoid receiving more information" condition is only an indirect proxy for epistemic avoidance because participants could ignore the post-task email. The Discussion mentions this, but for a leading journal the limitation should be elevated earlier and framed as a direct threat to interpretation.

[MAJOR] Confirmatory status | The study is not preregistered, yet many analyses are presented with fairly confirmatory language. A demanding editor will want a clearer distinction between preplanned core tests and exploratory follow-ups, especially for the moderated mediation and Bayesian model-selection components.

[MAJOR] Generalizability | The sample is a narrow student pool from Lyon, France, and the stimuli are brief non-partisan headlines. Claims about how "individuals" assess news in general, or how societies will respond to deepfakes and online misinformation, should be tightened.

A deep methods reviewer would likely say the paper is interesting and publishable in principle, but still vulnerable on construct validity: does the task measure information seeking about news ambiguity, or willingness to pay for a delayed message under a somewhat artificial incentive structure? To make the design convincing for a leading journal, the authors would need either a stronger causal handle on confidence or a more restrained, explicitly associational framing throughout.

### Part 3 - Analyses: Required and Suggested
**Required:**
1. [CRITICAL] Sensitivity analysis for the key confidence effect | Show that the confidence-to-information-seeking relationship remains substantively similar across alternative specifications that more aggressively partial out ambiguity, topic, wave, and participant-level response-style factors | A positive result would strengthen the claim that confidence carries explanatory weight beyond obvious shared determinants.
2. [CRITICAL] Stronger outcome-validation analysis | Quantify and discuss the extent to which willingness to pay to avoid information can be interpreted as epistemic avoidance rather than a pricing artifact or a low-stakes way to avoid later email noise | A positive result would make the central behavioral outcome easier to defend.
3. [CRITICAL] Sharper analysis of exploratory versus confirmatory claims | Separate the indispensable preregister-like core tests from secondary model comparisons and mediation analyses | A positive result would improve credibility and reduce concerns that the strongest interpretations rely on post hoc analytic layering.

**Suggested:**
1. [MAJOR] Heterogeneity analysis by baseline distrust, topic, and individual calibration | It matters because the theory implies stable metacognitive differences may shape information seeking | This appears feasible from the reported data.
2. [MAJOR] Trial-level robustness checks using simpler models alongside the main MLMs | It matters because some readers will trust transparent reduced-form estimates more than stacked model-selection logic | Feasible with the current dataset.
3. [MAJOR] Direct comparison of the predictive value of confidence versus raw ambiguity ratings | It matters because the paper argues for a metacognitive mechanism rather than a direct ambiguity heuristic | Feasible given the stimulus-level measures already exist.
4. [MAJOR] Additional analysis of the two collection waves | It matters because the waves span different calendar periods and misinformation contexts | Feasible and useful for robustness.

### Part 4 - Literature Positioning
The paper cites the right broad literatures: misinformation, confidence in perceptual decision-making, and information acquisition. The framing is strongest when it emphasizes ambiguous news as a case where calibration can fail while still influencing behavior. It is weaker when it drifts toward broad claims about misinformation spread, deepfakes, and societal implications that go beyond the direct evidence. The introduction would benefit from a more explicit contrast between this paper and adjacent work on truth bias, selective exposure, and information avoidance, so the paper's exact increment is unmistakable.

### Part 5 - Journal Fit and Recommendation
For a generic leading field journal, the paper is a plausible fit but not yet at the bar for immediate referral. The topic is timely, the design is coherent, and the results are interesting, but the paper currently overstates causal and mechanistic implications relative to what the design secures.

**Preliminary recommendation**: Revise before sending to referees

Concretely, the paper would need: tighter associational wording; a more explicit treatment of the main construct-validity limitation around the information-seeking measure; a cleaner separation of core and exploratory analyses; and robustness analyses showing the confidence effect survives stricter controls. Best realistic outlets, if the paper does not reach a top-field generalist bar, would be journals interested in misinformation, judgment and decision-making, or metacognition, including field-facing interdisciplinary outlets.

### Part 6 - Questions to the Authors
1. How do you justify language that confidence "drives" or "guides" information seeking when confidence is observed rather than experimentally manipulated?
2. What evidence shows that willingness to pay to avoid later information is a valid measure of epistemic avoidance rather than a preference about post-task burden or email engagement?
3. Which analyses were part of the original analytic plan, and which were added after inspection of the data?
4. How stable is the central confidence effect across the two data-collection waves when estimated separately?
5. Why should readers treat the moderated mediation model as mechanism-relevant rather than descriptive of correlated trial-level variables?
6. Which claims in the abstract and discussion should be read as causal, and which should be read as strictly associational?

## 2. Unsupported Claims & Experimental Design Integrity

## Agent 3: Unsupported Claims & Experimental Design Integrity

### Causal Overclaiming (must address)
1. [CRITICAL] Abstract and Discussion | "confidence in one's judgment was the primary driver of the demand for additional information about the news" | The design shows a strong association between confidence and information demand, but confidence was not experimentally manipulated, so "driver" implies more causal warrant than the data provide | Fix: replace with "confidence was the strongest observed predictor" or add causal evidence.
2. [CRITICAL] Abstract | "the demand for disambiguating information, driven by uncalibrated metacognition, became increasingly ineffective" | The moderated mediation model does not establish that uncalibrated metacognition causes ineffective information seeking | Fix: recast as an observed pattern consistent with that interpretation.
3. [MAJOR] Discussion | "this metacognitive sense of confidence was the most decisive dimension that guided information-seeking behavior" | "Guided" overstates what can be learned from a measured, nonrandomized predictor | Fix: use "was the strongest behavioral correlate" or similar.
4. [MAJOR] Conclusions | "confidence in one's judgment drives the willingness to seek additional information" | This repeats the same causal leap in the closing summary | Fix: replace with associational wording.

### Generalization Issues
1. [MAJOR] Discussion and Conclusions | Broad claims about "individuals" and "our societies" confronting misinformation and deepfakes | The sample is a French student pool, and the stimuli are brief non-partisan headlines | Fix: add an explicit population and task-boundary caveat when generalizing.
2. [MAJOR] Discussion | Implication that the findings characterize real-world news environments broadly | The task omits full articles, platform cues, source reputation, and social context | Fix: specify that the evidence is for headline-level judgments under controlled ambiguity.
3. [MINOR] Discussion | The paper occasionally implies durability of metacognitive traits from one session | The design is single-session and cannot support stable-trait claims | Fix: frame these as task-specific tendencies.

### Missing Caveats & Design Confounds
1. [CRITICAL] Outcome validity | The possibility that participants simply ignore post-task emails is acknowledged late, but it directly threatens the interpretation of "avoidance of information" | Where it should be addressed: Methods, Results framing, and Limitations | Suggested text: make clear that the behavioral measure indexes willingness to pay for later information delivery, not guaranteed later consumption.
2. [MAJOR] Confirmatory ambiguity | The study is not preregistered, yet several analyses are narrated as if confirmatory | Where it should be addressed: Methods and Results lead-in | Suggested text: distinguish hypothesis tests central to the design from exploratory follow-up models.
3. [MAJOR] Alternative explanation | Confidence may partly proxy item-level ambiguity or response fluency rather than a distinct metacognitive mechanism | Where it should be addressed: Results and Discussion | Suggested text: explicitly note this possibility and show robustness checks.

### Minor Language Issues
1. [MINOR] Abstract | "underscore the importance" | This is acceptable rhetorically, but still stronger than needed given the design | Fix: "suggest" or "indicate."
2. [MINOR] Discussion | "prime role" and "most decisive dimension" | These are stronger than the evidence warrants | Fix: use more neutral evaluative language.
